# Case Clarke v. Finch (1795)

## Case Summary

This repository serves as a simulated case file for a post-Revolutionary War land dispute, demonstrating the historical legal process involving a writ of *praecipe*.

### Facts of the Case

- **Plaintiff:** Thomas Clarke, a farmer residing in Pennsylvania.
- **Defendant:** Alistair Finch, a neighboring landholder.
- **Subject Matter:** A parcel of approximately 50 acres of farmland in Chester County, Pennsylvania.
- **Basis of Claim:** Thomas Clarke asserts ownership based on a colonial land grant issued in 1765 by the Proprietary Government of Pennsylvania. He alleges that Mr. Finch has wrongfully entered and occupied the land, refusing to vacate despite repeated demands.
- **Relief Sought:** Clarke seeks an action of ejectment to recover possession of the land, along with damages for loss of use and profits.

### Historical Context

The year is 1795, shortly after the adoption of the United States Constitution. Common-law writs, such as the *praecipe*, remained the primary method for initiating civil actions in many state courts. This case illustrates the procedural steps required to commence an ejectment action in Pennsylvania, relying on period‑accurate legal forms and terminology.

### Purpose

This simulation is designed for legal historians, practitioners, and students interested in the evolution of civil procedure, the role of writs in American legal history, and the practicalities of late‑18th‑century litigation.
